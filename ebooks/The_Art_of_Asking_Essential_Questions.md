[Dear Reader:]{.textStyle13}

[This miniature guide introduces the art of asking essential questions.
It is best used in conjunction with]{.textStyle13} [The Miniature Guide
to Critical Thinking]{.textStyle14} [and]{.textStyle13} [The Miniature
Guide to How to Study and Learn]{.textStyle14}[.]{.textStyle13}

[The quality of our lives is determined by the quality of our thinking.
The quality of our thinking, in turn, is determined by the quality of
our questions, for questions are the engine, the driving force behind
thinking. Without questions, we have nothing to think about. Without
essential questions, we often fail to focus our thinking on the
significant and substantive.]{.textStyle13}

[When we ask essential questions, we deal with what is necessary,
relevant, and indispensable to a matter at hand. We recognize what is at
the heart of the matter. Our thinking is grounded and disciplined. We
are ready to learn. We are intellectually able to find our way
about.]{.textStyle13}

[To be successful in life, one needs to ask essential questions:
essential questions when reading, writing, and speaking; when shopping,
working, and parenting; when forming friendships, choosing
life-partners, and interacting with the mass media and the
Internet.]{.textStyle13}

[Yet few people are masters of the art of asking essential questions.
Most have never thought about why some questions are crucial and others
peripheral. Essential questions are rarely studied in school. They are
rarely modeled at home. Most people question according to their
psychological associations. Their questions are haphazard and
scattered.]{.textStyle13}

[Essential questions fall into a range of categories. Some essential
questions are principally analytic, some principally evaluative. Some
apply predominantly to academic subjects, others to our innermost
thoughts, feelings, and desires.]{.textStyle13}

[As you might expect, the categories and lists of essential questions in
this mini-guide are illustrative, not exhaustive. Furthermore, the ideas
we provide are useful only to the extent that they are employed daily to
ask essential questions.]{.textStyle13} [Practice]{.textStyle14} [in
asking essential questions eventually leads to the]{.textStyle13}
[habit]{.textStyle14} [of asking essential questions. But we can never
practice asking essential questions if we have no conception of them.
This mini-guide is a starting place for understanding concepts that,
when applied, lead to essential questions.]{.textStyle13}

# []{#Introduction.xhtml#page_3 .page aria-label="3" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Introduction: The Power of Essential Questions]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub29) {.titlei}

[It is not possible to be a good thinker and a poor
questioner.]{.textStyle20}

[Questions define tasks, express problems, and delineate issues. They
drive thinking forward. Answers, on the other hand, offen signal a full
stop in thought. Only when an answer generates further questions does
thought continue as inquiry. A mind with no questions is a mind that is
not intellectually alive. No questions (asked) equals no understanding
(achieved). Superficial questions equal superficial understanding,
unclear questions equal unclear understanding. If your mind is not
actively generating questions, you are not engaged in substantive
learning.]{.textStyle20}

[Thinking within disciplines is driven, not by answers, but by essential
questions. Had no basic questions been asked by those who laid the
foundation for a field --- for example, physics or biology --- the field
would not have been developed in the first place. Every intellectual
field is born out of a cluster of essential questions that drive the
mind to pursue particular facts and understandings. Biology was born
when some humans pursued answers to the questions: "What are the
characteristics of living systems? What structures exist in them? What
functions do these structures serve?" Biochemistry was born when
biologists began to ask questions such as: "What chemical processes
underlie living things? How and why do chemical processes within living
things interact and change?"]{.textStyle20}

[Every field stays alive only to the extent that fresh questions are
generated and taken seriously as the driving force in thinking. When a
field of study is no longer pursuing significant answers to essential
questions, it dies as a field. To think through or rethink anything, one
must ask the questions necessary to thinking through the logic of that
thing, clearly and precisely.]{.textStyle20}

[In this miniature guide, we introduce essential questions as
indispensable intellectual tools. We focus on principles essential to
formulating, analyzing, assessing, and settling primary questions. You
will notice that our categories of question types are not exclusive.
There is a great deal of overlap between them. Deciding what category of
question to ask at any point in thinking is a matter of judgment. Having
a range of powerful questions to choose from is a matter of
knowledge.]{.textStyle20}

[Because we cannot be skilled at thinking unless we are skilled at
questioning, we strive for a state of mind in which essential questions
become second nature. They are the keys to productive thinking, deep
learning, and effective living.]{.textStyle20}

# []{#Part01.xhtml#page_5 .page aria-label="5" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Part One: Analytic Questions]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub30) {.titlep}

[Asking essential analytic questions is vital to excellence in thought.
When we analyze, we break a whole into parts. We do this because
problems in a "whole" are offen a function of problems in one or more of
its parts. Success in thinking depends, first of all, on our ability to
identify the components of thinking by asking essential questions
focused on those components.]{.textStyle20}

## [[Questioning the Structure of Thinking]{.textStyle22}](#nav.xhtml#sub31) {#Part01.xhtml#sub1 .noindent2}

[One powerful way to discipline your questions is to focus on the
components of reasoning, or parts of thinking. They are as
follows:]{.textStyle20}

[As you formulate questions, consider the following guidelines and
sample questions:]{.textStyle20}

[1.]{.textStyle20} [Questioning Goals and]{.textStyle23}
[Purposes]{.textStyle24}[. All thought reflects an agenda or purpose.
Assume that you do not fully understand someone's thought (including
your own) until you understand the agenda behind it. Questions that
focus on purpose in thinking include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What are we trying to accomplish
    here?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What is our central aim or task in this line of
    thought?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What is the purpose of this meeting, chapter,
    relationship, policy, law?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What is our central agenda? What other goals do
    we need to consider?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Why are we writing this? Who is our audience?
    What do we want to persuade them of?]{.textStyle20}

![chpt_fig_002](Images/chpt_fig_002.jpg){.scale60}

[2.]{.textStyle20} [Questioning]{.textStyle23}
[Questions]{.textStyle24}[.]{.textStyle20} [All thought is responsive to
a question. Assume that you do not fully understand a thought until you
understand the question that gives rise to it. Questions that focus on
questions in thinking include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [•]{.textStyle25} [I am not sure exactly what question you are
    raising. Could you explain it?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Is this question the best one to focus on at this
    point, or is there a more pressing question we need to
    address?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [The question in my mind is this... Do you agree
    or do you see another question at issue?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Should we put the question (problem, issue) this
    way... or that...?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [From a conservative viewpoint the question is
    ...; from a liberal viewpoint it is... Which is the most insightful
    way to put it, from your perspective?]{.textStyle20}

[]{#Part01.xhtml#page_6 .page aria-label="6"
role="doc-pagebreak"}[3.]{.textStyle20} [Questioning]{.textStyle23}
[Information]{.textStyle24}[, Data, and Experience]{.textStyle23}[. All
thoughts presuppose an information base. Assume that you do not fully
understand the thought until you understand the background information
(facts, data, experiences) that supports or informs it. Questions that
focus on information in thinking include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [•]{.textStyle25} [On what information are you basing that
    comment?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What experience convinced you of this? Could your
    experience be distorted?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [How do we know this information is accurate? How
    could we verify it?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Have we failed to consider any information or
    data we need to consider?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What are these data based on? How were they
    developed? Is our conclusion based on hard facts or soff
    data?]{.textStyle20}

[4.]{.textStyle20} [Questioning]{.textStyle23}
[Inferences]{.textStyle24} [and Conclusions]{.textStyle23}[. All thought
requires the making of inferences, the drawing of conclusions, the
creation of meaning. Assume that you do not fully understand a thought
until you understand the inferences that have shaped it. Questions that
focus on inferences in thinking include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [•]{.textStyle25} [How did you reach that conclusion?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Could you explain your reasoning?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Is there an alternative plausible
    conclusion?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Given all the facts, what is the best possible
    conclusion?]{.textStyle20}

[5.]{.textStyle20} [Questioning]{.textStyle23} [Concepts]{.textStyle24}
[and Ideas]{.textStyle23}[. All thought involves the application of
concepts. Assume that you do not fully understand a thought until you
understand the concepts that define and shape it. Questions that focus
on concepts in thinking include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What is the main idea you are using in your
    reasoning? Could you explain that idea?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Are we using the appropriate concept or do we
    need to re-conceptualize the problem?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Do we need more facts or do we need to rethink
    how we are labeling the facts?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Is our question a]{.textStyle20}
    [legal]{.textStyle14}[, a]{.textStyle20}
    [theological]{.textStyle14}[, or an]{.textStyle20}
    [ethical]{.textStyle14} [one?]{.textStyle20}

[6.]{.textStyle20} [Questioning]{.textStyle23}
[Assumptions]{.textStyle24}[. All thought rests upon assumptions. Assume
that you do not fully understand a thought until you understand what it
takes for granted. Questions that focus on assumptions in thinking
include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What exactly are you taking for granted
    here?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Why are you assuming that? Shouldn't we rather
    assume that...?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What assumptions underlie our point of view? What
    alternative assumptions might we make?]{.textStyle20}

[7.]{.textStyle20} [Questioning]{.textStyle23}
[Implications]{.textStyle24} [and Consequences]{.textStyle23}[. All
thought is headed in a direction. It not only begins somewhere (resting
on assumptions), it also goes somewhere (has implications and
consequences). Assume that you do not fully understand a thought unless
you know the most important implications and consequences that follow
from it. Questions that focus on implications in thinking
include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What are you implying when you
    say...?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [If we do this, what is likely to happen as a
    result?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Are you implying that ...?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Have you considered the implications of this
    policy (or practice)?]{.textStyle20}

[]{#Part01.xhtml#page_7 .page aria-label="7"
role="doc-pagebreak"}[8.]{.textStyle20} [Questioning]{.textStyle23}
[Viewpoints]{.textStyle24} [and Perspectives]{.textStyle23}[. All
thought takes place within a point of view or frame of reference. Assume
that you do not fully understand a thought until you understand the
point of view or frame of reference that places it on an intellectual
map. Questions that focus on point of view in thinking
include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [•]{.textStyle25} [From what point of view are you looking at
    this?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Is there another point of view we should
    consider?]{.textStyle20}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Which of these possible viewpoints makes the most
    sense given the situation?]{.textStyle20}

## []{#Part01.xhtml#page_8 .page aria-label="8" role="doc-pagebreak"}[Three Kinds of Questions]{.textStyle11} {.titles}

[In approaching a question, it is useful to figure out what type it is.
Is it a question with one definitive answer? Is it a question that calls
for a subjective choice? Or does the question require us to consider
competing answers?]{.textStyle20}

![chpt_fig_004](Images/chpt_fig_004.jpg){.scale70}

## []{#Part01.xhtml#page_9 .page aria-label="9" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Asking One System, No System, and Conflicting System Questions]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub32) {#Part01.xhtml#sub2 .titles}

[There are a number of essential ways to categorize questions for the
purpose of analysis. One such way is to focus on the type of reasoning
required by the question. With]{.textStyle38} [one system]{.textStyle39}
[questions, there is an established procedure or method for finding the
answer. With]{.textStyle38} [no system]{.textStyle39} [questions, the
question is properly answered in accordance with one's subjective
preference; there is no "correct" answer. With]{.textStyle38}
[conflicting system]{.textStyle39} [questions, there are multiple
competing viewpoints from which, and within which, one might reasonably
pursue an answer to the question. There are better and worse answers,
but no verifiable "correct" ones, since these are matters about which
even experts disagree (hence the "conflict" from system to
system).]{.textStyle38}

[Questions of Procedure (established- or one system) -]{.textStyle40}
[These include questions with an established procedure or method for
finding the answer. These questions are settled by facts, by definition,
or both. They are prominent in mathematics, as well as the physical and
biological sciences. Examples:]{.textStyle38}

-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What is the boiling point of lead?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What is the size of this room?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What is the differential of this
    equation?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [How does the hard drive on a computer
    operate?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What is the sum of 659 and 979?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [How is potato soup prepared, according to
    established Polish tradition?]{.textStyle38}

[Questions of Preference (no system) -]{.textStyle40} [Questions with as
many answers as there are different human preferences (a category in
which subjective taste rules). Examples:]{.textStyle38}

-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Which would you prefer, a vacation in the
    mountains or one at the seashore?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [How do you like to wear your hair?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Do you like to go to the opera? Which is your
    favorite?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What color scheme do you prefer in your
    house?]{.textStyle38}

[Questions of Judgment (conflicting systems) -]{.textStyle40} [Questions
requiring reasoning, but with more than one arguable answer. These are
questions that make sense to debate, questions with better-or-worse
answers (well-supported and reasoned or poorly-supported and/or
poorly-reasoned). Here we are seeking the best answer within a range of
possibilities. We evaluate answers to these questions using universal
intellectual standards such as clarity, accuracy, relevance, etc. These
questions are predominant in the human disciplines (history, philosophy,
economics, sociology, art...) Examples:]{.textStyle38}

-   [•]{.textStyle25} [How can we best address the most basic and
    significant economic problems of the nation today?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What can be done to significantly reduce the
    number of people who become addicted to illegal
    drugs?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [How can we balance business interest and
    environmental preservation?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Is abortion justifiable?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [How progressive should the tax system
    be?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [Should capital punishment be
    abolished?]{.textStyle38}
-   [•]{.textStyle25} [What is the best economic system?]{.textStyle38}

## []{#Part01.xhtml#page_10 .page aria-label="10" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Questioning Dogmatic Absolutism and Subjective Relativism]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub33) {#Part01.xhtml#sub3 .titles}

[Some people, dogmatic absolutists, try to reduce all questions to
matters of fact. They think that every question has one and only one
correct answer. Others, subjective relativists, try to reduce all
questions to matters of subjective opinion. They think that no question
has correct or incorrect answers but that all questions whatsoever are
matters of opinion: "I have my opinion and you have yours. Mine is right
for me and yours is right for you." Neither absolutist nor relativist
leaves room for what is crucial to success in human life: matters
of]{.textStyle20} [reasoned judgment]{.textStyle14}[.]{.textStyle20}

[Many important questions require our best judgment. It is required when
we sit on a jury, when we assess a political candidate, when we take
sides in a family argument, when we decide to support an educational
reform movement, when we decide on how to raise our children, how to
spend our money, or how much time to dedicate to public service.
Judgment based on sound reasoning goes beyond, but is never to be
equated with, fact or opinion alone. When one reasons well through
conflicting system questions, one does more than state facts.
Furthermore, a well-reasoned position is not to be described as mere
"opinion." We sometimes call a judge's verdict an "opinion," but we not
only expect, we demand, that it be based on relevant evidence and sound
reasoning.]{.textStyle20}

[When questions requiring reasoned judgment are reduced to matters of
subjective preference, counterfeit critical thinking occurs. Some
people, then, come to uncritically assume that everyone's "opinion" is
of equal value. Their capacity to appreciate the importance of
intellectual standards diminishes, and we can expect to hear comments
such as these: "What if I don't like these standards? Why shouldn't I
use my own standards? Don't I have a right to my own opinion? What if
I'm just an emotional person? What if I like to follow my intuition?
What if I think spirituality is more important than reason? What if I
don't believe in being "rational?" When people reject questions calling
for sound evidence and good reasoning, they fail to see the difference
between offering legitimate reasons and evidence in support of a view
and simply asserting the view.]{.textStyle20}

[Intellectually responsible persons, in contrast, recognize questions of
judgment for what they are: questions requiring the consideration of
alternative points of view. Put another way, intellectually responsible
persons recognize when a question calls for good reasoning (from
multiple points of view), and they behave in accordance with that
responsibility. This means that they realize when there is more than one
reasonable way to answer a question.]{.textStyle20}

[To determine which of these three types of questions we are dealing
with (in any given case) we can ask the following questions: Are there
relevant facts we need to consider? If yes, then either the facts alone
settle the question (and we are dealing with a question of procedure),
or the facts can be interpreted in different ways (and the question is
debatable). If there are no facts to consider, then it is a matter of
personal preference. Remember, if a matter is not one of personal
preference, then there must be some facts that bear on the question. If
the facts settle the question, then it is a "one system" procedural
question.]{.textStyle20}

## []{#Part01.xhtml#page_11 .page aria-label="11" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Questioning Concepts]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub34) {#Part01.xhtml#sub4 .titles}

[Concepts are ideas we use in thinking. They enable us to group things
in our experience in different categories, classes, or divisions. They
are the basis of the labels we give things in our minds. They represent
our mental map of the world telling us how things operate and what to
expect of them. Through our concepts we define situations, events,
relationships, and objects of our experience. Very important issues
depend on how we conceptualize things. For example, if we conceptualize
"animals" as having no rights, we may not consider it a matter of ethics
when they are made to suffer pain. We may take them to be nothing more
than "our property," to destroy or maintain as we please. Yet we have
the concept of "humane treatment" of animals. What concepts or ideas
does it depend upon? What does it presuppose? What does it imply? These
are conceptual questions.]{.textStyle20}

[Conceptual questions are questions settled through analysis of and/or
clarification of one or more concepts within a question. Conceptual
questions can be divided into at least two categories: simple and
complex.]{.textStyle20}

![chpt_fig_005](Images/chpt_fig_005.jpg){.scale70}

### [Simple Conceptual Questions (]{.textStyle41}[Definitional]{.textStyle42}[)]{.textStyle41} {.noindent}

[Simple conceptual questions are settled through the criteria implicit
in a standard definition of a word or phrase. To answer these questions,
we need merely to understand established meanings of words and how they
are properly applied to cases and circumstances. A beginning place for
addressing simple conceptual questions is a good dictionary. Consider
these examples:]{.textStyle20}

1.  [What are the basic differences between the meanings of the words
    'socialization,' 'training,' 'indoctrination,' and
    'education?']{.textStyle20}
2.  [Can a country be called a democracy if the political power is not
    in the hands of the people?]{.textStyle20}
3.  [Can you love a person and not care about her/his welfare and
    interests?]{.textStyle20}
4.  [If a government wages war on a civilian population, is it guilty of
    terrorism?]{.textStyle20}
5.  []{#Part01.xhtml#page_12 .page aria-label="12"
    role="doc-pagebreak"}[If a newspaper article on a contentious issue
    systematically implies that one side of the issue is correct, should
    it be considered unbiased?]{.textStyle20}
6.  [Is torture compatible with respecting one's basic
    rights?]{.textStyle20}

[Notice that each of these questions are settled as soon as one is clear
about the relevant meanings of the key words: socialization, training,
indoctrination, education, democracy, love, terrorism, and unbiased.
Unless one has good reason to question the established meanings, there
is little room for debate. To test what we are saying, look up each of
the key words, list the relevant meanings, and insert them into the
question in place of the key words.]{.textStyle20}

[Thus if democracy is a form of government in which the people rule,
then the question, "Can a country be called a democracy if the political
power is not in the hands of the people?" becomes "Can a country be said
to have]{.textStyle20} [a government in which the people
rule]{.textStyle14} [if the political power is not in the hands of the
people?" In this form, the question answers itself.]{.textStyle20}

[Complex Conceptual Questions]{.textStyle45}
[(]{.textStyle46}[Non-Definitional and
Multi-sided]{.textStyle42}[)]{.textStyle46}

[Now let us turn to complex conceptual questions. In this case, standard
definitions do not settle the question, but rather open the argument.
Divergent points of view can\
be brought to bear on the definitions stretching them this way or that.
Well-reasoned arguments can be devised from different standpoints.
Consequently, there are better and worse answers to complex conceptual
questions, but, at the present time, no "correct" or definitive answer
(see questions of judgment on page 9).]{.textStyle20}

[Consider these examples:]{.textStyle20}

1.  [To what extent is psychology scientific? To what extent is it
    not?]{.textStyle20}
2.  [Is democracy compatible with communism? Are there different forms
    of democracy? Of communism? Is democracy compatible with capitalism?
    What does each concept presuppose and imply? What must we consider
    to decide these questions?]{.textStyle20}
3.  [What is a true friend? Can you be a true friend to someone you
    dislike?]{.textStyle20}
4.  [What is the difference between love, friendship, and mere emotional
    attachment?]{.textStyle20}
5.  [Who is most responsible for the failure of the peace process in the
    Middle East?]{.textStyle20}
6.  [What countries in the world should be considered rogue
    states?]{.textStyle20}
7.  [Which of our laws are just and which unjust? And how does one
    decide?]{.textStyle20}

[To answer complex conceptual questions we need first to analyze the
ways educated persons use the concepts that guide the settling of the
questions. We need to figure out the most basic meanings of the terms
crucial to the questions.]{.textStyle20}

## []{#Part01.xhtml#page_13 .page aria-label="13" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Conceptual Tools for Conceptual Questions]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub35) {#Part01.xhtml#sub5 .titles}

[To analyze complex concepts we might use one or more of the following
strategies introduced by John Wilson (1963) in]{.textStyle38} [Thinking
With
Concepts]{.textStyle47}^[1](#Part01.xhtml#chr01.ftn.001){#Part01.xhtml#ch01.ftn.001}^[:]{.textStyle38}

[1)]{.textStyle38} [Focus on]{.textStyle39} [model]{.textStyle49}
[cases]{.textStyle39} [(paradigm instances of the concept). If we asked
the question "Are these two children being treated fairly by their
parents?" we might begin to address it by identifying cases in which
parents are investing equal resources and displaying equal concern for
both of their children. We would then examine the key characteristics of
these cases.]{.textStyle38}

[2)]{.textStyle38} [Focus on]{.textStyle39} [contrary]{.textStyle49}
[cases]{.textStyle39} [(examples depicting the opposite of the concept).
Considering contrary cases is useful because we can offen better
understand a concept through contrast, by considering cases that clearly
are not examples of the concept we are exploring. Focusing again on our
parent example above, we might look for contrary cases by identifying
situations where the children are clearly not being treated fairly by
their parents. We might consider a case, for example, in which one child
is clearly favored by the parents in contrast to the other. This might
be the case of a traditional first-born male. We would then examine the
leading characteristics of these cases in contrast to the model
cases.]{.textStyle38}

[3)]{.textStyle38} [Focus on]{.textStyle39} [related]{.textStyle49}
[cases]{.textStyle39} [(concepts and cases that function in relationship
with the concept we are exploring, are similar to our concept, or
importantly connected to it). With respect to our parenting question,
let's consider a similar case. To understand the concept of "equal
treatment" in parenting, we might consider the concept of "equal
treatment" in coaching. Imagine a coach who spends as much time
developing the unskilled players as he does the highly skilled players,
rather than favoring some over others. By considering this similar case,
we shed light on the concept of "equal treatment." Or, focusing on
concepts that function in relationship with another concept, consider
the question: What is a cell? To understand a cell, we need to
understand other related concepts, such as molecules, nucleotides, DNA,
RNA, enzymes, and proteins. We understand these concepts in relationship
with one another. We cannot fully understand one without understanding
the others. They are better understood as a group than
individually.]{.textStyle38}

[4)]{.textStyle38} [Focus on]{.textStyle39} [borderline]{.textStyle49}
[cases]{.textStyle39} [(cases with features both of the model case and
of cases ordinarily considered different from the model case). In a
borderline case, we feel inclined both to apply and withhold the
concepts. Again, focusing on our parenting example, let's say that one
of the two children is severely disabled and the parents therefore spend
the majority of their income on that child, rendering it impossible for
them to fund their other child's college education. This might be a
borderline case of "equal treatment" because the parents place the needs
of the disabled child over the needs of the other child, but they do so
for good reason. Or consider a different example. If a child touches a
stove and gets burned, the parent might say, "There, you got your
punishment!" Yet the parent doesn't mean that the child is literally
being punished, but that the child being burned is somewhat like
punishment. It has at least some features of punishment. Yet this case
would never be used as a dictionary example of the classic meaning of
punishment.]{.textStyle38}

[Deep conceptual questions are beyond the scope of this miniature guide.
Yet we can take Wilson's distinctions and, using them, briefly analyze a
few questions.]{.textStyle38}

[]{#Part01.xhtml#page_14 .page aria-label="14"
role="doc-pagebreak"}[Consider the question: Is it possible to attain
peace in the Middle East? In addressing this question, we need to know
how widely or how narrowly we are using the term "Middle East." This
should be a straight-forward stipulation ("By the Middle East I have in
mind...").]{.textStyle20}

[Once this is done, we can move to the more difficult analysis of the
concept of "peace" intended in the question. What degree or forms of
"peace" does one intend? What forms of "peace" can one imagine? What are
some model cases of "peace"? What are some contrary, related and
borderline cases? By "peace", do we mean all peoples living in
friendship, mutual respect, and mutual security (model case)? What other
concepts are intimately connected with "peace" (related cases)? Suppose
one country, being militarily superior, in effect fully conquers its
"enemies" imposing "peaceful" conditions on them (absence of overt
resistance or violation of imposed law). Would such a state be a state
of "peace"? Is "peace" consistent with mutual hatred (borderline case)?
Or suppose an agreement is reached in which those who sign for one of
the groups agree to conditions that the majority of its members reject
(borderline case)? Or suppose one of the groups is forced by vastly
inferior military power to accept conditions that are unjust (for
example, giving up much of their land and potential development) merely
to gain some level of freedom and self-government (borderline case)?
Would we consider any of these as achieving "peace?" To figure out what
we mean by "peace" we need to consider, in addition to a rich set of
cases, the context from which (and the history in which) this question
emerges. We need to consider, for example, the current structure of
power in the Middle East and the agendas of all of the participant
nations, what outcomes are possible and which of those, if any, warrant
the term "peace?"]{.textStyle20}

[There are no easy answers to complex conceptual questions, but
analyzing them helps us understand the nature and limits of our ideas.
We are, for example, a long way from understanding the concept of world
peace because its meaning is obfuscated by the machinations of power on
the one hand, and human irrationality on the other. For the powerful,
peace probably comes down to conditions under which their dominance is
quietly accepted. Peace then means their group getting what they want,
rightly or wrongly. There appears to be two conflicting logics at work:
the logic of peace (ideally speaking) and the logic of peace (in a world
of vastly unequal military and economic power).]{.textStyle20}

## []{#Part01.xhtml#page_15 .page aria-label="15" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Questioning Data, Information, and Experience]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub36) {#Part01.xhtml#sub6 .titles}

[Empirical questions are questions primarily answered through
determining facts. To answer an empirical question, we need to find out
the relevant facts: either by personal experience, research, or other
means. We raise empirical questions when we need knowledge about the
world and how things function in it.]{.textStyle20}

[Empirical questions fall into two categories: those for which the
answer has already been determined, and those not yet settled. When
dealing with the first type, we identify a reliable source or way to
settle the question. These we might call settled empirical questions.
The second type is any of those empirical questions that have not yet
been legitimately answered. Both depend on facts and our access to
them.]{.textStyle20}

::: box1
[Examples of]{.textStyle16} [settled empirical
questions]{.textStyle17}[:]{.textStyle16}

-   [■]{.textStyle52} [According to available statistics, how many
    people die each year from AIDS?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle54} [According to available statistics, how many
    children are annually sold into slavery?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle54} [According to available statistics, how many
    people are homeless in America?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle54} [Is there an effective vaccination for
    polio?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle54} [According to available statistics, how many
    children in the world die each day from malnutrition?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle54} [What are the major ways that electricity is
    generated?]{.textStyle16}

[Examples of]{.textStyle16} [unsettled empirical
questions]{.textStyle17}[:]{.textStyle16}

-   [■]{.textStyle54} [How can we cure AIDS?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle54} [Is it possible to achieve world peace (and put an
    end to war)?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle54} [Can a man ever grow to be 11-feet
    tall?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle54} [Given the rate of destruction to the ocean at the
    hands of humans, at what point will the ocean no longer be able to
    revive itself?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle54} [Is it possible to prevent money from being used
    in politics to serve the interests of the wealthy (at the expense of
    the larger public interest)?]{.textStyle16}
:::

[Sometimes we aren't sure whether an empirical question has been
settled. Consider the question: Can a horse jump a nine-foot fence?
There is already a host of information available on record-setting jumps
by horses. Through our research, we may find that a horse has already
jumped a nine-foot fence. If so, the question is settled. If not, we
must rely on available information and knowledge to reason through the
question. Considerable information is available about the physiology of
horses and the physics of jumping. It may or may not be possible for an
expert on both to come to a well-supported judgment about the
probability of some horse eventually jumping a nine-foot fence. Of
course, the question cannot be affirmatively settled until some horse
actually jumps the height.]{.textStyle20}

[Many questions are not exclusively empirical, but have an important
empirical dimension. In that case we need to determine which part of the
question is factual, what facts, precisely, are relevant, and how to
locate those facts.]{.textStyle20}

[Whether settled or unsettled, empirical questions, of course, can also
be either simple or complex.]{.textStyle20}

## []{#Part01.xhtml#page_16 .page aria-label="16" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Questioning Questions: Identifying Prior Questions]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub37) {#Part01.xhtml#sub7 .titles}

[Whenever we are dealing with complex questions, one tool useful in
disciplining our thinking is that of identifying questions presupposed
in a question that is our direct concern. In other words, because
questions offen presuppose other questions having been answered, it is
offen useful to question a question by figuring out what "prior"
questions it assumes, or, alternatively, what other questions it would
be helpful for us to answer first, before we try to answer the immediate
question at issue. This is especially important when dealing with
complex questions. We can offen approach a complex question through
simpler questions.]{.textStyle20}

[Hence, to answer the question "What is multiculturalism?" it would be
helpful to first settle the question, "What is culture?" And to settle
that question, it would be helpful to answer the question, "What are the
factors about a person (nationality, religion, ideology, place of
birth...) that determine what culture he or she belongs
to?"]{.textStyle20}

[To construct a list of prior questions, begin by writing down the main
question you are focused on. Then formulate as many questions as you can
think of that you would have to answer, or it would be helpful to
answer, before answering the first. Then take this list and determine
what question or questions you would have to answer, or it would be
helpful to answer, prior to answering these questions. Continue,
following the same procedure for every new set of questions on your
list.]{.textStyle20}

[As you proceed to construct your list, keep your attention focused on
the first question on the list as well as on the last. If you do this
well, you should end up with a list of questions that shed light on the
logic of the first question.]{.textStyle20}

::: box2
[Main question:]{.textStyle56} [What is history?]{.textStyle16}

[Prior questions:]{.textStyle56}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent do all historians share the same
    goal?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is it possible to include all relevant facts of
    the past in a history book?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [How many of the events during a given time period
    are left out in a history of that time period?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is more left out than is included?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [How does a historian know what to
    emphasize?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Can historical value judgements be
    objective?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What variables might influence a historian's
    viewpoint?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is it possible to simply list facts in a history
    book or does all history writing involve interpretations as well as
    facts?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is it possible to decide what to include and
    exclude and how to interpret facts without adopting a historical
    point of view?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [How can we begin to evaluate a historical
    interpretation?]{.textStyle16}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [How can we begin to evaluate a historical point
    of view?]{.textStyle16}
:::

## []{#Part01.xhtml#page_19 .page aria-label="0" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Questioning in Decision-Making and Problem-Solving]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub40) {#Part01.xhtml#sub10 .titles}

[Everyday life is an endless sequence of decisions. Some decisions are
small and inconsequential; some large and life-determining. When we
consistently make rational decisions, we live a rational life. When we
consistently make irrational decisions, we live an irrational life.
Rational decisions maximize the quality of one's life without violating
the rights or harming the well-being of others. Rational decisions
maximize our chances of happiness, successful living, and
fulfillment.]{.textStyle64}

[The same points can be made for problem-solving. Our daily lives are
filled with problem-solving situations. The better we are at solving
problems, the more fulfilled and rational are our lives.]{.textStyle64}

[The Logic of Decision-Making]{.textStyle17}

[To make rational decisions, we need to use our understanding of the
logic of decision-making to routinely ask questions that improve the
quality of our decisions. Through our questions, we raise the process of
decision-making to the level of conscious and deliberate
choice.]{.textStyle64}

[The logic of decision-making, then, is determined by the need to make a
decision and the consequences that follow from that need.]{.textStyle65}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [The goal:]{.textStyle66} [to decide between some
    set of alternatives, the one most in keeping with our welfare and
    the welfare of others.]{.textStyle65}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [The question:]{.textStyle66} ["At this point in
    my life, faced with the alternatives (A or B or C or D), which is
    the one most likely to enhance my welfare and the welfare of
    others?"]{.textStyle65}

[Four keys to sound decision-making are:]{.textStyle65}

1.  [To recognize when you face an important decision]{.textStyle65}
2.  [To accurately identify the alternatives]{.textStyle65}
3.  [To logically evaluate the alternatives]{.textStyle65}
4.  [To act on the best alternative]{.textStyle65}

[Good thinkers routinely ask the following types of questions when
making decisions:]{.textStyle65}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What should be my main goal in making this
    decision?]{.textStyle65}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [From what point of view am I looking at this
    decision?]{.textStyle65}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What is the precise question I am trying to
    answer?]{.textStyle65}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What information do I need to answer this
    question?]{.textStyle65}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What can I safely assume in reasoning through
    this decision?]{.textStyle65}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What are my alternatives in this
    situation?]{.textStyle65}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What are some likely implications of my deciding
    this versus deciding that?]{.textStyle65}

[The Logic of Problem-Solving]{.textStyle17}

[Most of the points we made about decision-making can also be made about
problem-solving. Problems are embedded in the fabric of our lives almost
to the same extent as decisions. Every domain of decision-making is also
a domain in which we have to solve problems. Every decision has an
impact on our problems, either to minimize them or to contribute to
them. Poor decisions create problems. Many problems can be avoided by
sound decision-making early on.]{.textStyle64}

[Problems can be divided into two types:]{.textStyle65}

1.  [Problems that we ourselves have created by our own decisions and
    behavior.]{.textStyle65}
2.  [Problems created by forces outside of us.]{.textStyle65}

[Let us then divide each of these into two groups:]{.textStyle65}

1.  [Problems that we can solve, in whole or in part.]{.textStyle65}
2.  [Problems beyond our control.]{.textStyle65}

[Clearly, we are apt to have the best chance of solving problems that we
ourselves have created, for we offen have the capacity to reverse
decisions we previously made and modify behavior in which we previously
engaged.]{.textStyle64}

## []{#Part01.xhtml#page_20 .page aria-label="20" role="doc-pagebreak"}[Guidelines and Guideline Questions for Effective Problem Solving]{.textStyle11} {.titles}

1.  [Figure out and regularly rearticulate your goals, purposes, and
    needs. Recognize problems as emergent obstacles to reaching your
    goals, achieving your purposes, and satisfying your needs. Ask these
    questions:]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What are my most important goals? What
        barriers, if any, exist to my achieving those
        goals?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What goal is this problem keeping me from
        achieving?]{.textStyle65}
2.  [Identify your problems explicitly; then analyze them. Wherever
    possible, take problems one by one. Ask these
    questions:]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What precisely is the problem? Is the problem
        multidimensional? Do I need to break the problem into multiple
        problems and analyze each one separately?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What kind of problem is this?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What sorts of things will I have to do to
        solve the problem?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is this a problem I have created? If so, how
        can I avoid creating problems such as this in the
        future?]{.textStyle65}
3.  [Figure out the information you need, and actively seek that
    information. Ask these questions:]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What information do I need to solve the
        problem?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [How can I obtain the
        information?]{.textStyle65}
4.  [Carefully analyze, interpret, and evaluate the information you
    collect, drawing reasonable inferences. Ask these
    questions:]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What are the various ways I could reasonably
        interpret the information relevant to the issue?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Have I open-mindedly considered the various
        ways of analyzing the information, or am I refusing to consider
        some information? In other words, am I being
        close-minded?]{.textStyle65}
5.  [Figure out your options for action and evaluate them. Ask these
    questions:]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What can I do in the short term? In the long
        term?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What parts of the problem are under my
        control? What parts are not?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [How am I limited by money, time, and
        power?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What are my options? What are the advantages
        and disadvantages of each option?]{.textStyle65}
6.  [Adopt a strategic approach to the problem and follow through on
    that strategy. This may involve direct action or a carefully
    thought-through, wait-and-see strategy. Ask these
    questions:]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [How can I approach this problem in the most
        logical way possible?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Should I deal with it immediately, or should
        I slowly and carefully approach the problem?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What are the advantages of each
        approach?]{.textStyle65}
7.  [When you act, monitor the implications of your action as they begin
    to emerge. Be ready at a moment's notice to revise your strategy if
    the situation requires it. Be prepared to shift your strategy or
    your analysis or statement of the problem, or all three, as more
    information about the problem becomes available to you. Ask these
    questions:]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What implications would follow if I decided
        to act in this way versus in that way?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Have I really solved the problem, or does it
        still exist?]{.textStyle65}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I need to change my approach to the
        problem; change my strategy?]{.textStyle65}

## [[Determining Value, Merit, and Worth]{.textStyle22}](#nav.xhtml#sub42) {#Part02.xhtml#sub11 .noindent6}

[Evaluative questions are questions that call on us to determine the
value, worth or quality of something or someone. Assessment is integral
to virtually all human experience and can be done well or poorly. Our
ability to effectively evaluate what is going on and how to act in a
situation is directly determined by the quality of the questions we ask
in the situation.]{.textStyle20}

::: boxs
[Essential Questions for Assessment]{.textStyle67}

1.  [What am I assessing and why?]{.textStyle68}
2.  [What precise questions am I trying to answer?]{.textStyle68}
3.  [What information will I need to adequately complete this
    assessment?]{.textStyle68}
4.  [What criteria or standards will I use in the assessment
    process?]{.textStyle68}
5.  [Are there any potentially negative implications of my mode of
    evaluation?]{.textStyle68}
6.  [Is my plan for evaluation logical, realistic, and
    practical?]{.textStyle68}
:::

[Two Types of Evaluative Questions]{.textStyle22}

[All evaluative questions fall into two categories: one system and
conflicting system. Evaluative questions are not to be confused with
questions of preference (for which subjective choice is the determining
factor).]{.textStyle20} [See page 9 for an introduction to the three
types of questions.]{.textStyle14}

::: boxg
[Evaluative questions (that can be definitively
answered):]{.textStyle22}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Given industry ratings, what are the best windows
    for withstanding strong winds?]{.textStyle64}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Which automobile has the best survival rate in
    head-on collisions?]{.textStyle64}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Given industry standards, is this a good hammer
    for general carpentry?]{.textStyle64}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What is the quality of air in the Los Angeles
    area?]{.textStyle64}

[Evaluative questions (that call for reasoned judgment between
conflicting views):]{.textStyle22}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What type of car should I buy?]{.textStyle64}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What type of refrigerator will best suit our
    needs?]{.textStyle64}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [How should we design this house so as to meet the
    needs of the family?]{.textStyle64}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Should I pursue a new career?]{.textStyle64}

## []{#Part02.xhtml#page_22 .page aria-label="22" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Evaluating Reasoning (Overall)]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub43) {#Part02.xhtml#sub12 .titles}

[Educated and reasonable thinkers use intellectual standards to assess
reasoning. These standards include, but are not limited
to,]{.textStyle20} [clarity, precision, accuracy, relevance, depth,
breadth, logicalness, and fairness]{.textStyle23}[. Skilled thinkers
routinely ask questions specifically targeting these
standards.]{.textStyle20}

1.  [Questioning Clarity]{.textStyle23}[. Thinking is always more or
    less]{.textStyle20} [clear]{.textStyle70}[. Questions that focus on
    clarity in thinking are:]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Could you elaborate on what you are
        saying?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Could you give me an example or illustration
        of your point?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [I hear you saying "x." Am I hearing you
        correctly, or have I misunderstood you?]{.textStyle20}
2.  [Questioning Precision]{.textStyle23}[. Thinking is always more or
    less]{.textStyle20} [precise]{.textStyle70}[. Questions that focus
    on precision in thinking are:]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Could you give me more details about
        that?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Could you be more specific?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Could you specify your concerns more
        fully?]{.textStyle20}
3.  [Questioning Accuracy]{.textStyle23}[. Thinking is always more or
    less]{.textStyle20} [accurate]{.textStyle14}[. Questions that focus
    on accuracy in thinking are:]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [How could we check that to see if it is
        true?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [How could we verify these alleged
        facts?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Can we trust the accuracy of these data given
        the source from which they came?]{.textStyle20}
4.  [Questioning Relevance]{.textStyle23}[. Thinking is always capable
    of straying from the task, question, problem, or issue under
    consideration. Questions that focus on relevance in thinking
    are:]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [I don't see how what you said bears on the
        question. Could you show me how it is relevant?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Could you explain what you think the
        connection is between your question and the question we have
        focused on?]{.textStyle20}
5.  [Questioning Depth]{.textStyle23}[. Thinking can function either at
    the surface of things or probe beneath that surface to deeper
    matters and issues. To figure out whether a question is deep, we
    need to determine whether it involves complexities. Questions that
    focus on depth in thinking are:]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is this question simple or complex? Is it
        easy or difficult to answer?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What makes this a complex question? Are
        conflicting points of view relevant?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [How are we dealing with the complexities
        inherent in the question?]{.textStyle20}
6.  []{#Part02.xhtml#page_23 .page aria-label="23"
    role="doc-pagebreak"}[Questioning Breadth]{.textStyle23}[. Thinking
    can be broad or narrow.]{.textStyle20} [Breadth]{.textStyle70} [of
    thinking requires the reasoner to think insightfully within more
    than one point of view or frame of reference. Questions that focus
    on breadth in thinking are:]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What points of view are relevant to this
        issue?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [What relevant points of view have we
        considered?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Am I failing to consider this issue from an
        opposing perspective because I am not open to changing my
        view?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Have I entered the opposing views in good
        faith, or only enough to find flaws in them?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [I have looked at the question from an
        economic viewpoint. Does it have an ethical
        dimension?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [I have considered a liberal position on the
        issue. What would conservatives say?]{.textStyle20}
7.  [Questioning Logic]{.textStyle23}[. Thinking is more or
    less]{.textStyle20} [logical]{.textStyle70}[. Questions that focus
    on logic are:]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Does all this make sense
        together?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Does your first paragraph fit in with your
        last? Does what you say follow from the evidence?]{.textStyle20}
8.  [Questioning Fairness]{.textStyle23}[. Thinking can be more or
    less]{.textStyle20} [fair]{.textStyle70}[. Questions that focus on
    fairness are:]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I have any vested interest in this
        issue?]{.textStyle20}
    -   [■]{.textStyle57} [Am I sympathetically representing the
        viewpoints of others?]{.textStyle20}

## []{#Part02.xhtml#page_24 .page aria-label="24" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Evaluating Reasoning (The Parts)]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub44) {#Part02.xhtml#sub13 .titles}

[In addition to questioning a text analytically, we can question it
evaluatively by applying intellectual standards to the elements of
reasoning:]{.textStyle20}

1.  [Focusing on the author's]{.textStyle71} [Purpose]{.textStyle72}[:
    Is the author's purpose well-stated or clearly implied? Is it
    justifiable?]{.textStyle71}
2.  [Focusing on the key]{.textStyle71} [Question]{.textStyle72} [which
    the written piece answers: Is the question at issue well-stated (or
    clearly implied)? Is it clear and unbiased? Does the expression of
    the question do justice to the complexity of the matter at issue?
    Are the question and purpose directly relevant to each
    other?]{.textStyle71}
3.  [Focusing on the most important]{.textStyle71}
    [Information]{.textStyle72} [presented by the author: Does the
    writer cite relevant evidence, experiences, and/or information
    essential to the issue? Is the information accurate? Does the writer
    address the complexities of the issue?]{.textStyle71}
4.  [Focusing on the most fundamental]{.textStyle71}
    [Concepts]{.textStyle72} [at the heart of the author's reasoning:
    Does the writer clarify key ideas when necessary? Are the ideas
    relevant and significant?]{.textStyle71}
5.  [Focusing on the author's]{.textStyle71}
    [Assumptions]{.textStyle72}[: Does the writer show a sensitivity to
    what he or she is taking for granted or assuming? Does the writer
    use questionable assumptions without addressing problems inherent in
    those assumptions?]{.textStyle71}
6.  [Focusing on the most important]{.textStyle71}
    [Inferences]{.textStyle72} [or conclusions in the written piece: Do
    the inferences made and conclusions drawn clearly follow from the
    information relevant to the issue, or does the author jump to
    unjustifiable conclusions? Does the author consider alternative
    conclusions where the issue is complex? Can you identify any flaws
    in the reasoning?]{.textStyle71}
7.  [Focusing on the author's]{.textStyle71} [Point of
    View]{.textStyle72}[: Does the author show a sensitivity to
    alternative relevant viewpoints or lines of reasoning? Does s/he
    consider and respond to objections from other relevant points of
    view?]{.textStyle71}
8.  [Focusing on]{.textStyle71} [Implications]{.textStyle72}[: Does the
    writer display a sensitivity to the implications and consequences of
    the position s/he is taking?]{.textStyle71}

[One of the most common problems in addressing complex questions arises
when the question at issue is unclear. When the question is unclear or
vague, thinking has no clear guide. It wanders without a clear sense of
relevance. Thoughts are scattered. But when we take time to clarify a
question, we are better able to settle it. We make clear to ourselves
the intellectual task at hand and what that task requires of
us.]{.textStyle20}

[One of the most effective strategies is to add details to the question,
to break it down, and to be more exact. We disentangle questions best
treated separately. We notice relations and overlap between
distinguishable sub-questions.]{.textStyle20}

[Essential Idea:]{.textStyle17} [A question is clear when we know

## []{#Part02.xhtml#page_26 .page aria-label="26" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Questioning As We Read]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub46) {#Part02.xhtml#sub24 .titles1}

[Skilled readers are able to master a subject from books alone, without
benefit of lectures or class discussion. It is possible to become
educated through reading alone. Skilled readers actively question as
they read. They question to understand. They question to evaluate what
they are reading. They question to bring important ideas into their
thinking.]{.textStyle20}

[Skilled readers approach reading as an active dialogue involving
routine questioning. Here are some of the questions critical readers ask
while reading:]{.textStyle20}

1.  [■]{.textStyle57} [Why am I reading this? What is my purpose? What
    do I want to gain?]{.textStyle20}
2.  [■]{.textStyle57} [What is the author's purpose? What can I learn
    about the author's point of view by care fully reading the title as
    well as the preface, introduction, and table of
    contents?]{.textStyle20}
3.  [■]{.textStyle57} [From paragraph to paragraph, can I summarize what
    the author is saying in my own words? What questions do I
    have?]{.textStyle20}
4.  [■]{.textStyle57} [Is there some part of this sentence or paragraph
    that I don't understand? What part am I unsure of? What part am I
    clear about?]{.textStyle20}
5.  [■]{.textStyle57} [If I do not understand something, is the author
    vague or is there a problem with my reading?]{.textStyle20}
6.  [■]{.textStyle57} [What questions do I have? How important is it for
    me to get these questions answered before reading
    further?]{.textStyle20}
7.  [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I understand the meaning of key terms, or do I
    need to look them up in a dictionary?]{.textStyle20}
8.  [■]{.textStyle57} [Is the author using key words in standard or
    extraordinary ways?]{.textStyle20}
9.  [■]{.textStyle57} [Are the facts presented credible, or should I
    question them?]{.textStyle20}
10. [■]{.textStyle57} [What are the most significant ideas in this
    text?]{.textStyle20}
11. [■]{.textStyle57} [How is what I am reading relevant to me? How can
    I connect it to what matters or will matter in my
    life?]{.textStyle20}
12. [■]{.textStyle57} [What is the nature of the question at issue in
    the text? Do I clearly understand the complexities in
    it?]{.textStyle20}
13. [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I need to investigate the issue further in
    some other text before moving forward in this one?]{.textStyle20}

[Analyzing an Author's Reasoning Through Questions]{.textStyle22}

[Purpose]{.textStyle17}[: What is the author's purpose?]{.textStyle20}

[Question]{.textStyle17}[: What question is the author attempting to

[Information]{.textStyle17}[: What information does the author use in

[Inferences]{.textStyle17}[: What are the main conclusions or inferences

[Concepts]{.textStyle17}[: What are the main ideas that guide the

[Assumptions]{.textStyle17}[: What does the author take for

[Point of View]{.textStyle17}[: What is the author looking at, and how

[Implications]{.textStyle17}[: If the author is correct (or insightful),

## []{#Part02.xhtml#page_27 .page aria-label="27" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Questioning As We Write]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub47) {#Part02.xhtml#sub15 .titles}

[To write well is to produce written work that is both clear and
well-reasoned. To achieve this end, the skilled writer routinely asks
questions of self-analysis and assessment.]{.textStyle38}

[Analyzing Your Reasoning Before Writing]{.textStyle22}

[Prior to writing, it is important to understand clearly the logic of
your position. You can best determine this by focusing on the elements
of reasoning. You can ask the following types of
questions:]{.textStyle38}

[Purpose]{.textStyle62} [: What is my purpose in writing this
piece?]{.textStyle38}

[Question]{.textStyle62} [: What question(s) am I attempting to
answer?]{.textStyle38}

[Information]{.textStyle62} [: What information do I need to support my
position?]{.textStyle38}

[Inferences]{.textStyle62} [: What do I want to convince the reader
of?]{.textStyle38}

[Concepts]{.textStyle62}[: What are the main ideas I need to use in my
thinking to effectively write this piece? Which of these ideas do I need
to explicitly define and elaborate?]{.textStyle38}

[Assumptions]{.textStyle62}[: Should I question what I am taking for
granted in writing this piece? Should I explicitly state my
assumptions?]{.textStyle38}

[Point of View]{.textStyle62}[: What am I looking at, and how am I
seeing it? Should I include other viewpoints?]{.textStyle38}

[Implications]{.textStyle62}[: What am I implying? What am I trying to
get the reader to believe and/or do?]{.textStyle38}

[Evaluating Your Writing As You Write]{.textStyle72}

[To write well, you also need to evaluate as you write.]{.textStyle38}

[Clarity]{.textStyle62}[: Am I clear about what I am saying or is my
thinking muddled? For each paragraph, have I stated my main idea, then
elaborated it? Have I provided examples to make my points clear? Have I
illustrated important ideas? Have I written sentences that can be
interpreted in different ways or have I made my intended meaning
clear?]{.textStyle38}

[Precision]{.textStyle62}[: Have I provided adequate details for the
reader to understand precisely what I mean? Do I need more
details?]{.textStyle38}

[Logic]{.textStyle62}[: Do all the ideas in my paper fit logically
together? Have I used transitional words to make connections between
ideas evident to the reader?]{.textStyle38}

[Relevance]{.textStyle62}[: In the paper as a whole, do I keep a clear
and consistent focus? Do I wander from the main point? In each
paragraph, is everything in the paragraph relevant to the main idea in
the paragraph?]{.textStyle38}

[Significance]{.textStyle62}[: What is the most significant question to
focus on? What are the most significant concepts? Facts?]{.textStyle38}

[Depth]{.textStyle62}[: Do I clearly understand what makes the issue
complex? Have I sufficiently detailed those complexities?]{.textStyle38}

[Fairness]{.textStyle62}[: Have I been fair to all viewpoints relevant
to the issue or have I presented opposing viewpoints in a "weak" form in
order to dismiss them?]{.textStyle38}

[Accuracy]{.textStyle62}[: Have I made sure that all the information I
have presented as factual is so? Are my sources of information
credible?]{.textStyle38}

[Once you have completed a written piece, you can then use the template
on page 24 to evaluate your own reasoning, just as you would to evaluate
any author's reasoning.]{.textStyle38}

## []{#Part02.xhtml#page_28 .page aria-label="28" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Asking Ethical Questions]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub48) {#Part02.xhtml#sub16 .titles}

[Ethics is the study of what benefits or harms persons and creatures.
Human behavior can be either ethically praised (if someone acts to
benefit the welfare of others) or criticized (when someone acts so as to
harm others). Ethics is not to be confused with social convention, law,
or religious beliefs. Unethical acts deny another person or creature an
inalienable right. Social convention and laws, as well as religious
beliefs, vary enormously along national and cultural lines. All ethical
questions are settled in accordance with ethical concepts and principles
that do not so vary. Ethical questions can be either simple or
complex.]{.textStyle20}

[The following classes of harmful acts enable us to define universal
rights:]{.textStyle20}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [SLAVERY: Enslaving people, whether individually
    or in groups.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [GENOCIDE: Systematically killing with the attempt
    to eliminate a whole nation or ethnic group.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [TERRORISM: Waging war against
    civilians.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [TORTURE: Inflicting severe pain as an act of
    revenge or to obtain information from a person.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [SEXISM: Treating people unequally (and harmfully)
    in virtue of their gender.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [RACISM: Treating people unequally (and harmfully)
    in virtue of their race or ethnicity.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [MURDER: The pre-meditated killing of people for
    revenge, pleasure, or to gain advantage.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [ASSAULT: Attacking an innocent person with intent
    to cause grievous bodily harm.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [RAPE: Forcing an unwilling person to have
    intercourse.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [FRAUD: Intentional deception to cause someone to
    give up property or some right.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [DECEIT: Representing something as true which one
    knows to be false in order to gain a selfish end harmful to
    another.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [INTIMIDATION: Forcing a person to act against his
    interest or deter from acting in his interest by threats or
    violence.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Putting persons in jail without telling them the
    charges against them or providing them with a reasonable opportunity
    to defend themselves.]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Putting persons in jail, or otherwise punishing
    them, solely for their political or religious views.]{.textStyle20}

[Simple ethical questions]{.textStyle22} [are questions involving
clear-cut ethical principles. Some examples:]{.textStyle76}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is it cruel to subject an innocent creature to
    unnecessary suffering?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is it unjust to deny someone a basic human
    right?]{.textStyle20}
-   []{#Part02.xhtml#page_29 .page aria-label="29"
    role="doc-pagebreak"}[■]{.textStyle57} [Is it ethically wrong to
    cheat or deceive people?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is it ethically wrong to torture people in order
    to exact a confession?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is it ethically wrong to use another person to
    serve your selfish interests?]{.textStyle20}

[Complex ethical questions]{.textStyle22} [are questions that can be
argued in more than one way (using ethical principles). Some
examples:]{.textStyle76}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Under what conditions, if any, should animal
    experimentation be allowed?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is it ethically wrong to kill animals for
    food?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent should scientists be allowed to
    experiment with new viruses (when the virus they create might itself
    cause harm)?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Under what conditions should people be kept
    artificially alive?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do scientists have a special ethical
    responsibility to society?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Are we ethically justified in engaging in
    unethical practices in our own defense because our enemies use them
    against us?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent are we ethically obligated to
    contribute to the healthfulness of the environment?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Under what conditions, if any, is capital
    punishment ethically justifiable?]{.textStyle20}

[Distinguishing Among Questions of Ethics, Questions of Cultural
Preference, and Questions of Religion]{.textStyle22}

[Ethical questions are offen confused with questions from other domains,
for example, social conventions, religion and the law. People commonly
believe that social conventions, laws, and religious beliefs are
self-evidently ethical. Yet social norms, religious theology, and laws
all may (inadvertently) advocate unethical behavior. People can be
socially ostracized or imprisoned for behavior that is not ethically
wrong. Many religions have been used to justify such ethically repugnant
practices as racism and slavery.]{.textStyle20}

[The following examples highlight confusions of ethics with religion,
law, or social conventions:]{.textStyle20}

[Confusing ethics and the law:]{.textStyle22}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Many sexual practices (such as homosexuality)
    have been unjustly punished with life imprisonment or death (under
    the laws of one society or another).]{.textStyle20}

## []{#Part03.xhtml#page_34 .page aria-label="34" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Questioning the Status of Disciplines]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub52) {#Part03.xhtml#sub19 .titles}

[When studying any discipline, it is important to determine the
strengths and weaknesses in it. To do this you must question the status
of knowledge and "expert" information in the field, rather than blindly
accepting what you read and are told about the discipline. Of course,
you must do this through disciplined and responsible thinking, being
alert to both strengths and weaknesses. Some critical questions to ask
about a field of study are:]{.textStyle38}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent do competing schools of thought
    exist within this field?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent do experts in this field disagree
    about the answers they give to important questions?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What other fields deal with this same subject,
    from a different standpoint perhaps? To what extent do conflicting
    views exist about this subject in light of these different
    standpoints?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent, if at all, is this field properly
    called a science?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent can questions asked in the field
    be answered definitively? To what extent are questions in this field
    matters of (arguable) judgment?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent does public pressure influence
    professionals in the field to compromise their professional practice
    in light of public prejudice or vested interest?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent is it likely that professionals
    within the discipline will act in accordance with their vested or
    selfish interest, rather than in a fair-minded way? What types of
    "opportunities" exist for professionals within the field to serve
    their own interest in lieu of serving those they purport to
    serve?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What does the history of the discipline tell us
    about the status of knowledge in the field?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [How old is the field? How common is controversy
    over fundamental terms, theories, and orientation?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [How wide is the likely gap between the promised
    ideal of instruction in the discipline and the actual
    results?]{.textStyle38}

[Some Critical Questions to Ask About a Textbook:]{.textStyle43}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [If there are competing schools of thought within
    this field, what is the orientation of the textbook writers? Do they
    highlight these competing schools and detail the implications of
    that debate?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Are other textbooks available that approach this
    field from a significantly different standpoint? If so, how should
    we understand the orientation or bias of this
    textbook?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Would other experts in this field disagree with
    any of the answers to important questions given in this textbook?
    How would they disagree?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Are there textbooks in other fields dealing with
    this same subject, from a different standpoint perhaps? To what
    extent do conflicting views exist about this subject in the light of
    these different standpoints?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent does this textbook represent this
    field as a science? If so, do some experts in the field disagree

    science?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent do the questions asked in this
    textbook lead to definitive answers? Conversely, to what extent are

[[Asking Essential Questions in the Social
Disciplines]{.textStyle22}](#nav.xhtml#sub55)

[The social disciplines include academic courses that foster

[We can ask questions that are outwardly oriented, such as those in the

## [[Questioning Ourselves as Learners]{.textStyle22}](#nav.xhtml#sub58) {#Part04.xhtml#sub25 .noindent7}

[Questioning ourselves as learners is essential to deep learning.
Internalize the following questions, and routinely ask them of
yourself.]{.textStyle20}

[Idea #1:]{.textStyle25} [Do I understand the requirements of every
class I am taking, how they will be taught and what will be expected of
me? Have I sought out and received advice about how I can best prepare
for class?]{.textStyle20}

[Idea #2:]{.textStyle25} [Do I know my strengths and weaknesses as a
student and thinker? Have I tried to find out? Am I in the habit of
evaluating aspects of my thinking --- my purpose, the question I am
trying to answer, the information I am using to answer it, etc.? Can I
distinguish what I know for sure from what I merely believe (but may not
be true)?]{.textStyle20}

[Ide]{.textStyle26}[a #3:]{.textStyle25} [Have I identified the KIND of
thinking that is most important in a given class? Think of subjects as
forms of thinking (History = historical thinking; Sociology =
sociological thinking; Biology = biological thinking).]{.textStyle20}

[Ide]{.textStyle26}[a #4:]{.textStyle25} [Do I ask questions in and out
of class? Do I engage myself in lectures and discussions by asking
questions?]{.textStyle20}

[Ide]{.textStyle26}[a #5:]{.textStyle25} [Am I looking for
interconnections? Do I understand the content in every class as a SYSTEM
of interconnected understandings? Or do I just memorize random facts
like a parrot? Do I study to understand, to figure things
out?]{.textStyle20}

[Idea #6:]{.textStyle25} [Am I practicing the thinking of the subject?
Can I explain this thinking with examples and illustrations (to someone
who is not in the class)?]{.textStyle20}

[Idea #7:]{.textStyle25} [Am I reading my textbooks to figure out the
THINKING of the author(s)? Do I translate the author's thinking into my
thinking (by putting basic points into my own words)? Do I role-play the
author (to someone else) explaining the main points of the various
sections of the text?]{.textStyle20}

role="doc-pagebreak"}[Ide]{.textStyle26}[a #8:]{.textStyle25} [Do I
relate content whenever possible to issues and problems and practical
situations in my life?]{.textStyle20}

[Idea #9:]{.textStyle25} [Can I explain the main idea behind the class
in my own words? Am I seeking to find the key concept of the course from
the first couple of class meetings? For example, in a Biology course,
try explaining what biologists are (mainly) trying to figure out. Don't
use technical terms in your explanation. Then relate that explanation to
each segment of what you are learning in the course. How does each
segment fit in?]{.textStyle20}

[Ide]{.textStyle26}[a #10:]{.textStyle25} [Do I test myself before I
come to class by trying to summarize, orally or in writing, the main
points of the previous class meeting? If I cannot summarize main points,
I haven't learned them.]{.textStyle20}

[Ide]{.textStyle26}[a #11:]{.textStyle25} [Do I check my thinking using
intellectual standards? Am I being clear? Accurate? Precise? Relevant?
Logical? Am I looking for what is most significant? Am I recognizing
complexities?]{.textStyle20}

[Ide]{.textStyle26}[a #12:]{.textStyle25} [Do I use writing as a way to
learn by writing summaries in my own words of important points from the
textbook or other reading material? Can I construct test questions and
write out my answers to them?]{.textStyle20}

[Ide]{.textStyle26}[a #13:]{.textStyle25} [During lecture, do I actively
listen for main points? If we arbitrarily stopped the lecture at various
points, could I accurately summarize what the instructor had just said
in my own words?]{.textStyle20}

[Ide]{.textStyle26}[a #14:]{.textStyle25} [Do I frequently assess my
reading? Do I read the textbook actively? Am I asking questions as I
read? Do I recognize the points I do and do not
understand?]{.textStyle20}

## []{#Part04.xhtml#page_40 .page aria-label="40" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Questioning Our Egocentrism]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub59) {#Part04.xhtml#sub26 .titles}

[One of the primary barriers to the development of insightful thinking
is the natural human tendency toward egocentric thought. Humans
naturally see the world in self-serving terms. In other words, we do not
naturally consider the rights and needs of others, nor do we naturally
appreciate the point of view of others or the limitations in our own
point of view. As humans we become explicitly aware of our egocentric
thinking only if specially trained to do so. We do not naturally
recognize our egocentric assumptions, the egocentric way we use
information, the egocentric way we interpret data, the source of our
egocentric concepts and ideas, the implications of our egocentric
thought. We do not naturally recognize our self-serving
perspective.]{.textStyle20}^[2](#Part04.xhtml#chr04.ftn.002){#Part04.xhtml#ch04.ftn.002}^

[One of the great barriers to detecting egocentric thought comes from
the self-deceptive nature of the human mind. Through self-deception,
humans live with the unrealistic but confident sense that we have
fundamentally figured out the way things actually are, and that we have
done this objectively. We naturally believe in our intuitive perceptions
--- however inaccurate. In other words, though human thinking is offen
flawed, it nevertheless sees itself as right, correct, in possession of
"the truth." To take command of our egocentric tendencies, we need to
actively target these tendencies through questions. In other words, we
need to routinely question our motivations and study our own
"selfishness" and narrowmindedness.]{.textStyle20}

## []{#Part04.xhtml#page_41 .page aria-label="41" role="doc-pagebreak"}[Formulating Questions that Target Egocentrism]{.textStyle11} {.titles}

[By focusing on the two motives of egocentric thinking, we can formulate
questions that target our own egocentrism, questions specifically
designed to uncover selfishness and self-validation. Here are some
examples:]{.textStyle20}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I usually consider the views of those who
    disagree with me? Do I tend to assume that those who disagree with
    me are wrong?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I tend to place my needs and desires over the
    needs and desires of others?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [When I have something personal to gain, does my
    fairness to others diminish?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Will I personally gain something for myself in
    this situation if I ignore or distort some information or
    viewpoint?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Am I usually willing to consider that I might be
    wrong?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I tend to ignore information that would
    require me to rethink my position?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I tend to assume that I know more than I
    actually do?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I assert information to be true when I don't
    know for sure that it is?]{.textStyle20}

[We can also question the motives of others, through questions such
as:]{.textStyle20}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is this other person considering my rights and
    needs, or the rights and needs of others?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is he using me to serve his selfish
    interest?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is she distorting what I am saying? If so, why?
    Does she have something to gain by doing so?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is he trying to manipulate me?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is she honestly trying to understand what I am
    saying? Is she able to accurately state what I am trying to
    say?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is she willing to admit she might be
    wrong?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is he open to reason? Or is he
    close-minded?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is she refusing to consider relevant information
    in order to maintain her viewpoint?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is he assuming that he knows more than he
    does?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is she asserting something as true that may not
    be?]{.textStyle20}

[Egocentrism and Power]{.textStyle22}

[One of the natural motives of the human mind is the desire for power.
All of us need some power. If we are powerless, we are unable to satisfy
our needs. Without power, we are at the mercy of others. Hence, the
acquisition of power is essential for human life. But we can pursue
power through either rational or irrational means, and we can use power
to serve rational or irrational ends. Power used irrationally is
typically justified egocentrically.]{.textStyle20}

## []{#Part04.xhtml#page_42 .page aria-label="42" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Questioning Our Sociocentrism]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub60) {#Part04.xhtml#sub27 .titles}

[Living a human life entails membership in a variety of human groups.
This typically includes groups such as nation, culture, profession,
religion, family, and peer group. We find ourselves participating in
groups before we are aware of ourselves as living beings. What is more,
every group to which we belong has some social definition of itself and
some (usually unspoken) "rules" guiding the behavior of all members.
Each group to which we belong imposes some level of conformity on us as
a condition of acceptance. This includes a set of beliefs, behaviors,
and taboos.]{.textStyle20}

[For most people, blind conformity to group restrictions is automatic
and unreflective. Most conform without recognizing their conformity.
They internalize group norms and beliefs, take on group identities, and
act as "expected" --- without the least sense that what they are doing
might reasonably be questioned. Most people function in social groups
unreflectively assuming the rightness of the system of beliefs,
attitudes, and behaviors to which they conform.]{.textStyle20}

[This conformity of thought, emotion, and action is not restricted to
the masses, or the lowly, or the poor. It is characteristic of people in
general, independent of their role in society, independent of status and
prestige, independent of years of schooling. Conformity of thought and
behavior is the rule in human life;independence and non-conformity, the
rare exception.]{.textStyle20}

[Sociocentric thinking, as we intend this expression, is egocentric
thinking raised to the level of the group. It is as destructive as
egocentric thinking, if not more so, as it carries with it the sanction
of a social group. When sociocentric thinking is made explicit in the
mind of the thinker, its unreasonableness is generally evident. However,
just as individuals deceive themselves through egocentric thinking,
groups deceive themselves through sociocentric thinking. Just as
egocentric thinking functions to serve one's selfish interest,
sociocentric thinking functions to serve the selfish interests of the
group. Just as egocentric thinking operates to validate the uncritical
thinking of the individual, sociocentric thinking operates to validate
the uncritical thinking of the group.]{.textStyle20}

[Questions we can ask to target our sociocentric
tendencies:]{.textStyle22}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What groups do I belong to and how do they
    influence my behavior when I am with the group? How do these groups
    influence my behavior when I am away from the group?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Is it in my best interest to belong to these
    groups?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What does this group require of its members (its
    demands)?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What behaviors does this group forbid (its
    taboos)?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What behaviors are allowed within the group (its
    range of free decision)?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What would happen to me if I went against the
    taboos of the group or culture? Would I be ostracized? Would I be
    imprisoned? Would I be killed?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [How does my society influence my
    behavior?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What is involved in thinking like an American (a
    German, a Japanese person)?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Have I ever thought within the perspective of
    another culture?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What beliefs and behaviors does my culture
    punish? Are these forbidden behaviors unethical, or are they
    culturally relative?]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What would happen to me if I violated any of the
    taboos of my culture?]{.textStyle38}

## []{#Part04.xhtml#page_43 .page aria-label="43" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Questioning to Develop Intellectual Dispositions]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub61) {#Part04.xhtml#sub28 .titles}

[To cultivate ourselves as fair-minded, intellectually responsible
persons, we strive to develop intellectual virtues or dispositions.
These attributes are essential to excellence of thought. They determine
with what insight and integrity we think.]{.textStyle20}

[This section contains brief descriptions of the intellectual virtues,
along with related questions that foster their
development.]{.textStyle20} [Only to the extent that you are routinely
asking these questions of yourself are you developing these
virtues]{.textStyle14}[.]{.textStyle20}

[Intellectual humility]{.textStyle90} [is knowledge of ignorance;
sensitivity to what you know and what you do not know. It means being
aware of your biases, prejudices, self-deceptive tendencies and the
limitations of your viewpoint. Questions that foster intellectual
humility include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [What do I really know (about myself, about the
    situation, about another person, about my nation, about what is
    going on in the world)?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent do my prejudices or biases
    influence my thinking?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [How do the beliefs I have uncritically accepted
    keep me from seeing things as they are?]{.textStyle20}

[Intellectual courage]{.textStyle90} [is the disposition to question
beliefs you feel strongly about. It includes questioning the beliefs of
your culture and the groups to which you belong, and a willingness to
express your views even when they are unpopular. Questions that foster
intellectual courage include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent have I analyzed the beliefs I
    hold?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent have I questioned my beliefs, many
    of which I learned in childhood?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent have I demonstrated a willingness
    to give up my beliefs when sufficient evidence is presented against
    them?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent am I willing to stand up against
    the majority (even though people ridicule me)?]{.textStyle20}

[Intellectual empathy]{.textStyle90} [is awareness of the need to
actively entertain views that differ from our own, especially those we
strongly disagree with. It is to accurately reconstruct the viewpoints
and reasoning of our opponents and to reason from premises, assumptions,
and ideas other than our own. Questions that foster intellectual empathy
include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent do I accurately represent
    viewpoints I disagree with?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Can I summarize the views of my opponents to
    their satisfaction? Can I see insights in the views of others and
    prejudices in my own?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I sympathize with the feelings of others in
    light of their thinking differently than me?]{.textStyle20}

[Intellectual integrity]{.textStyle90} [consists in holding yourself to
the same intellectual standards you expect others to honor (no double
standards). Questions that foster intellectual integrity
include:]{.textStyle20}

-   []{#Part04.xhtml#page_44 .page aria-label="44"
    role="doc-pagebreak"}[■]{.textStyle57} [Do I behave in accordance
    with what I say I believe, or do I tend to say one thing and do
    another?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent do I expect the same of myself as
    I expect of others?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent are there contradictions or
    inconsistencies in my life?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent do I strive to recognize and
    eliminate self-deception in my life?]{.textStyle20}

[Intellectual perseverance]{.textStyle90} [is the disposition to work
your way through intellectual complexities despite the frustration
inherent in the task. Questions that foster intellectual perseverance
include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Am I willing to work my way through complexities
    in an issue or do I tend to give up when I experience
    difficulty?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Can I think of a difficult intellectual problem
    concerning which I have demonstrated patience and determination in
    working through the difficulties?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I have strategies for dealing with complex
    problems?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I expect learning to be easy or do I recognize
    the importance of engaging in challenging intellectual
    work?]{.textStyle20}

[Confidence in reason]{.textStyle90} [is based on the belief that one's
own higher interests and those of humankind at large are best served by
giving the freest play to reason. It means using standards of
reasonability as the fundamental criteria by which to judge whether to
accept or reject any belief or position. Questions that foster
confidence in reason include:]{.textStyle20}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Am I willing to change my position when the
    evidence leads to a more reasonable position?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I adhere to principles of sound reasoning when
    persuading others of my position or do I distort matters to support
    my position?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I deem it more important to "win" an argument,
    or see the issue from the most reasonable
    perspective?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I encourage others to come to their own
    conclusions or do I try to force my views on them?]{.textStyle20}

[Intellectual autonomy]{.textStyle90} [is thinking for oneself while
adhering to standards of rationality. It means thinking through issues
using one's own thinking rather than uncritically accepting the
viewpoints of others. Questions that foster intellectual
autonomy:]{.textStyle20}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent am I a conformist?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [To what extent do I uncritically accept what I am
    told by my government, the media, my peers?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Do I think through issues on my own or do I
    merely accept the views of others?]{.textStyle20}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Having thought through an issue from a rational
    perspective, am I willing to stand alone despite the irrational
    criticisms of others?]{.textStyle20}

# []{#Conclusion.xhtml#page_46 .page aria-label="46" role="doc-pagebreak"}[[Conclusion: Questioning Systematically and Socratically]{.textStyle11}](#nav.xhtml#sub62) {.titles}

[When we take command of our thinking, we do so through our questions.
We move beyond questions that are undisciplined, fragmented and random.
We turn from merely questioning to questioning systematically,
comprehensively, and with breadth of vision. This mode of questioning is
sometimes called "Socratic" questioning. The Socratic questioner deeply
probes thinking. What the word "Socratic" adds to ordinary questioning,
then, is systematicity, depth, and a keen interest in assessing the
truth or plausibility of things.]{.textStyle20}

[One of the primary goals of developing thinkers is to establish a
disciplined, "executive" component in their thinking, a powerful inner
voice of reason, to monitor, assess, and repair --- in a more rational
direction --- their thinking, feelings, and action. Socratic questioning
provides that inner voice. Here are some of the fundamentals of Socratic
questioning, followed by examples of questions you might ask in
beginning to probe the thinking of another person.]{.textStyle20}

-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Seek to understand --- when possible --- the
    ultimate foundations for what is said or believed, and follow the
    implications of those foundations through further questions. (You
    might ask, for example, "On what do you base your beliefs? Could you
    explain your reasoning to me in more detail so I can more fully
    understand your position?")]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Recognize that any thought can exist fully only
    in a network of connected thoughts. Therefore, treat all assertions
    as a connecting point to further thoughts. Pursue those connections.
    (You might ask, for example, "If what you say is true, wouldn't x or
    y also be so?")]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Treat all thoughts as in need of development.
    (You might ask: "Could you elaborate on what you are saying so I can
    better understand you?")]{.textStyle38}
-   [■]{.textStyle57} [Recognize that all questions presuppose prior
    questions and all thinking presupposes prior thinking. When raising
    questions, be open to the questions they presuppose. (You might ask,
    for example, "To answer this complex question, what other questions
    do we need to answer?")]{.textStyle38}

[There are many more ways in which we can systematically question what
we are learning, what we are reading, writing, saying, what we think and
why we think what we do, what others say and what they might mean. Some
of these questions have been the focus of this miniguide. Our goal is to
practice using these questions offen enough that they become second
nature to us, so that we become instinctive questioners, those with a
disposition to ask the right questions, the essential questions. If we
do, we will gain greater and greater command of our thinking, and
through that command, command of our emotions and our lives as
well.]{.textStyle20}
